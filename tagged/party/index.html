Paris New Year (in Brooklyn!)

When my youngest daughter was 10 months old and my oldest was 2 1/2, our family of 4 became a family of 3.  As tough as it was initially I quickly learned to embrace the role as solo parent to my 2 little girls.  

Shortly after relocating from Manhattan to Brooklyn I joined a local single parent support group.  It was there that I first encountered the concept of “Paris New Year,” — celebrating midnight on Paris time — which translates to 6pm East Coast time.  It was the perfect way to bring in the New Year with young children, as they are awake for all of the fun and excitement and mommy could still celebrate *and* be asleep by midnight to prepare for their early morning rise.  That first Paris New Year was one of the most memorable evenings of my life, as I was surrounded by dozens of people who understood the new journey I had embarked on, and, more importantly: I would later go on to marry the host of the party, himself a parent with custody of his 2 boys! 

We continue the tradition of Paris New Year to this day, now as a family of 6, though we don’t always host anymore.  The tradition has spread like wildfire among our close friends.  If you prefer not to call a sitter and head out for an adults-only evening, Paris New Year may be just the thing for you.  And if you do decide to host yourself, be forewarned that your fellow parents will come in droves, so make it easy on yourself and declare it a potluck!

As long as you have some bubbly on hand (don’t forget sparkling apple cider or some other delightfully bubbly drink for the kids to toast with) you shouldn’t feel obligated to carry the French theme over to the menu.  Anything goes. 

I’ll post some menu ideas and some kiddie mocktail recipes shortly (inspired by the discovery of preservative and dye-free Maraschino cherries)!

Halloween: Savory Tricks & Sweet Treats

Pictured above:  Ghost shaped sharp cheddar cheese slices and whole grain crackers — great as an after-school snack, a classroom party offering or on a Halloween party buffet table.


Above:  Hand-carved clementine jack-o-lanterns, the ultimate in-classroom party treat.  Last year we made around 50 of these and they flew off their trays faster than a witch making a hasty getaway on her broom. 

Halloween bento:  Mini chicken pot pies (recipe to be posted Wednesday, October 12) and seasonal fruit (honeycrisp apple slices; hand-carved clementine)


Halloween dinner:  Individual shepherd’s pie (made from ground turkey and vegetables and a mix of yukon gold mashed potatoes and whipped sweet potatoes — recipe to be posted this Friday, October 14).  This idea was inspired by a post by Gina Homolka.


Delicious, moist whole wheat pumpkin muffins with added flax other healthy goodness…with dark chocolate chunks (recipe to be posted this Thursday, October 13).  Don’t underestimate the punch they pack in mini form!

_________________________________________________________

Ahhh….Halloween, an absolute favorite holiday of mine.  I love everything about it, except maybe the excess, and only then because I want the kids to indulge but not end up with tummy aches galore (plus there is that little dietary issue of ours, and lets face it — there are plenty of other holidays and birthdays right behind this one, so even kids who aren’t on a “restrictive diet” could probably do well with a gentle reining in). 

We *love* trick or treating, good candy and raging around the neighborhood for hours on end, but…  there has to be a method behind the madness in order for me to fully enjoy the beauty of it all.  Oh yes, my 5 point criteria before we rage is as follows:

  • A healthy and filling home-packed lunch at school
  • A nutritious after-school snack, and healthy party contributions should there be a classroom party
  • A hearty dinner that is portable and easy to eat, because the kids are always way too excited to park themselves down at the dinner table and eat a full sit-down meal before heading out to trick-or-treat.  Otherwise, if they don’t eat before they leave the house, they’ll naturally want to devour everything in their path.
  • An at-home high-quality candy stash for everyone to trade up to, should they wish (and my kids usually do prefer high quality dark chocolate over things like Snickers Bars).
  • To have the “Switch Witch” (wink, wink — they’re on to that one big time, but still go along with it) come to our home at midnight and work her magic, switching up all the candy the kids don’t want for a much longed-for present wrapped in shiny black paper with orange bows.  The kids place their offering to her near the front door, and bye-bye goes the nastiness (or at least a good chunk of it).

In short, it is absolutely possible to enjoy all the fun that Halloween has to offer while still managing to maintain some semblance of sanity.

Deconstructed Tri-berry Shortcake

This year we’re celebrating the 4th of July at my cousin’s annual poolside bash in New Jersey, and my job is to bring a relatively healthy dessert that will appeal to the masses.  Relatively healthy on the 4th of July?  Yup.  But relax.  We plan on hanging out with the grillmaster so we’ll be in enough trouble as it is.  Trust me.  Plus we want to be able to sample whatever is brought by the other guests. 

After much deliberation while staving off a serious cake craving, I settled on my offering: a deconstructed tri-berry shortcake that is big on berries and has just enough cubed cake and whipped cream to make it festive, topped with a dye-free maraschino cherry.  On the day-of I plan to serve it layered in a big glass bowl.  My 15 year old stepson tested the version that was photographed and enthusiastically announced that he approves wholeheartedly!

Happy St. Patrick’s Day !

Shamrock-shaped tea sandwiches (light cream cheese and thinly sliced cucumber) on whole wheat bread with a sprinkling of edible spinach shamrock confetti garnish; Irish flag fruit salad (green honeydew melon balls, orange cantaloupe, white anjou pear balls); snapea crisps and pistachios

For today’s bento I wanted to steer clear of holiday themed sweets, opening up the possibility of having something special for dessert tonight with company, like a low-fat, home-made version of a mini shamrock shake.  I also wanted to avoid green food dye in favor of something from Mother Nature.  The girls came up with the idea for the tea sandwiches on their own, but the meal seemed a bit lackluster to me.  That’s when I saw an alert for a new post by Jessica on FOOD MAYHEM, and viola —I found a way to inject a bit of cuteness.

I absolutely fell in love with Jessica’s basil concept (and wondered how the heck she managed to come up with it while caring for a brand new baby).  I did decide to switch it up a bit and go with spinach instead, as the girls will eat it raw. As the luck of the Irish would have it, I happened to be in close proximity of Paper Presentation at that very moment so I was able to pick up the four leaf clover paper punch without any headaches.  Or so I thought.  The headache came later, as the paper punch is actually very difficult to work with while using leafy greens. 

The fruit salad was originally honeydew and anjou pears, but my husband suggested that we add cantaloupe to complete the colors of the Irish flag.  In a large clear bowl, Irish flag fruit salad would be great at an at-home or classroom party.

The end result of today’s bento is festive (although Jessica managed to ROCK that paper cutter, whereas I barely squeaked by), and according to the girls has a nice “yum” factor to it, especially since they are melon-a-holics.
