A Teenage Babysitter, a Fast No-Cook Meal Plan & Harry Potter

Tonight the Little Ladies will have their first experience with a teenage babysitter flying solo (their older step-brothers are away this week).  They are over-the-moon excited, and they have a whole list of things they’d like to do in the 4 whole hours their parents will be away.  Luckily for the babysitter, the list includes the 2nd installment in the Harry Potter series (Harry Potter and the Chamber of Secrets), so it should really be an easy gig.

With their father and I dining out, I decided to give myself a much-deserved break and let the kids order in.  What do children with familial hypercholesterolemia eat when ordering take-out anyway?  Or for that matter, what does any child whose parents strive to feed them a healthy (or at least healthier) diet order?  Well, we’ve figured a bunch of valid options, but tonight they’re having GASP! pizza, a fruit & veggie tray and root beer floats (the latter being a more benign version of the typical special treat served in ice cream parlors). 

Here is the plan:

Main Course: 

Pizza - Readers of this blog know that we typically have “Pizza Friday,” especially at lunch time during the school year when we mimic the school cafeteria and serve the same lunch item, only much healthier versions.  Check the archives for examples.  I do not subscribe to the thought that pizza has to have extra cheese and a load of meat (like sausage and pepperoni) to be yummy, nor do typically feel good about blowing the day’s allotment of saturated fat, sodium and calories in one sitting (on the rare occasions that I do it has to be on something extra special). Pizza can, in fact, be very healthy.

Tonight we will order a thin crust whole wheat margherita pizza from our local pizzeria, and request that the fresh mozzarella topping be reduced to half of what they normally serve.  The pizzeria also serves homemade sauce (without excess sugar or sodium) and uses extra virgin olive oil, so we’re good there.  The kids prefer their veggies on the side (not on) their slices, and that’s perfectly fine.  I, however, love my veggies piled sky high!

Beverage - Filtered water.  It’s what we typically drink with dinner every night.

Side Dish: 

Fruit & Cheese Platter - a simple-to-manage selection of dark cherries, watermelon slices (pre-cut by me), whole pluots and raw vegetables such as baby carrots, red and orange peppers and cucumbers and whatever else they pull out of the fridge.

And for the finale:

Dessert: 

Root Beer Floats - Using smaller glasses (like these from Crate and Barrel, only ours are 4 ounces), the root beer float will be made from Oogave Root Beer (click here for information on the brand) which contains agave as a sweetener, and a scoop of low-fat vanilla frozen yogurt.  One 12 ounce bottle can be split between 3 glasses, so it’s a lot of yum and not much yuck.

I have a feeling my kids are going to enjoy themselves tonight.  I have a feeling their parents will too.

School Ice Cream Party

Pictured:  My 8 year old daughter’s choice for her ice cream sundae:  Haagen-Dazs low-fat vanilla frozen yogurt, chunks of frozen black cherries and a drizzle of melted dark chocolate.

The end of the school year is coming to a close, and we’ve taken part in one celebration after another — end of the school year picnics, pizza parties, breakfasts and a slew of summer birthdays celebrated early.  Needless to say, there has been a lot of extra food, as the celebrations are always in addition to (never in lieu of) regular lunch and snack.

Honestly, the amount of junk food at school over the past couple of weeks has been excessive for any child,  but for the child on a restrictive diet it has been a minefield.

I have observed the feeding frenzy with particular curiosity because our school has reworked their cafeteria menu, formed a Wellness Committee, maintains a working school garden, and the students are fresh off of “Food Detectives Week” where they learn about making healthy food choices.  These are all good, amazing things that I support wholeheartedly, but any sort of change in the actual classroom has not been obvious.

Some of the issues we’ve faced all year long but are currently getting slammed with this week:

  • Politicizing.  Food is a highly politicized topic, and in my experience, if you appeal to other parents (and even some of the teachers) to cut back on the junk food they are likely to feel judged or simply ignore you, even when you converse in the most polite, light-hearted manner possible. “Cutting back” somehow equates with “banning fun.”
  • Escalation.  What begins as a “breakfast party” at 8:20 in the morning is bound to include at least 2 varieties of cake and a box of Munchkins once the contribution sheet is passed around.  Another example:  our “ice cream sundae party” (ice cream with every topping you can think of) has now grown to include full-size cupcakes.
  • Isolation.  You do not want your child to feel socially isolated.  In order to navigate around this we make exceptions here and there.  Even on a restrictive diet, life is too short not to indulge on occasion, and at other times we provide healthier substitutions.  I have been known to bring in beautifully decorated fruit and low-fat cheese sculptures for school holiday parties and, even with cake and candy present, the fruit and cheese is consumed at the speed of light.  For an ice cream party though (and post lunch and snack at that), you’d probably be the odd man out with that sort of contribution.
  • Education.  In general, people agree that most foods are ok in moderation, but the discrepancy comes with the definition of moderation (frequency of consumption, portion size, etc.).  Everyone wants to do the right thing for their kids, but even the experts can’t seem to agree on what that right thing would be.

Yes, it’s painfully obvious that we are a long way away from getting a handle on the amount of sugar and fat and processed junk our kids are exposed to in what should be “neutral” territory. 

I am personally convinced that we can have school celebrations that include food that is delicious, beautiful and fun — without all the junk in excess — so that when we do want to indulge in something sinful we can do so on our own terms and not have to second guess what may have been consumed at our children’s desks. 

If your school has experienced any success in achieving a balance you are happy with, I’d love to hear about it.  I am specifically interested in learning about school birthday parties/in-classroom holiday celebrations and the like.  I would particularly love to hear from those who have very young children who aren’t yet of the age where they can make their own choices wisely, and from the parents of older kids who are transitioning to a healthier diet at this stage in their lives.

I’ll be sharing some of our own successful experiments at summer’s end as we gear up for another school year, and would love to include some of yours!

Introduction

Little Ladies Who Lunch was inspired by a diagnosis of FAMILIAL HYPERCHOLESTEROLEMIA for 3 out of 6 household members (myself and my 2 young daughters) just days before the start of the 2010-2011 school year.

The panic that ensued quickly gave way to "when life gives you lemons, make lemonade." And preserved lemons. And lemon roasted chicken. And low-fat lemon yogurt cake. You get the picture.
