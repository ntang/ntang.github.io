The Last of the Leftovers & Thanksgiving Wrap-Up

Unlike most families, ours never seems to tire of turkey.  Today’s bento features the last of the turkey breast thinly sliced and served on pretzel bread (a special treat) smeared with a little homemade cranberry sauce with hints of citrus.  The sides consist of fresh kiwi, naval orange slices with pomegranate seeds and a salad of diced red bell peppers and cucumber. 

Thanksgiving was an extraordinary feast for 14 after rising early to do our local 5 mile Turkey Trot.  The whole family including our kids ages 6, 8, 12 and 16 participated.  We felt like champions (walkers/runners and eaters)! 

My favorite part of Thanksgiving was watching all of the children involve themselves in the prep.  My 6 year old grated cheeses.  My 8 year old made the homemade cranberry sauce using half water and half fresh squeezed orange juice, sugar (not as much as previous years) and grated clementine rind.  My 16 year old stepson made pumpkin pie for the first time using a recipe from Kitchen Vignettes. And my 12 year old stepson acted as a runner and pinch-hitter on a little bit of everything, including walking and entertaining the dog and running out for last minute forgotten items so the rest of us could continue cooking and cleaning. 

All in all, it was a wonderful time.

Recipe: Individual Shepherd’s Pies

For the meat filling* (ingredients):

  • 2 tablespoons extra virgin olive oil
  • 1 cup chopped onion
  • 2 carrots, peeled and diced very small
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 1/2 pounds ground meat (we used free-range ground turkey)
  • 1 teaspoon kosher salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon freshly ground black pepper
  • 2 tablespoons all-purpose flour
  • 2 teaspoons tomato paste
  • 1 cup organic chicken broth
  • 2 teaspoons Worcestershire sauce
  • 2 teaspoons freshly chopped rosemary leaves
  • 1 teaspoon freshly chopped thyme leaves
  • 1/2 cup fresh or frozen corn kernels
  • 1/2 cup fresh or frozen English peas

*tweaked slightly from Alton Brown’s recipe.  Note:  feel free to substitute or add vegetables to taste.

Directions:

Place the olive oil into a 12-inch saute pan and set over medium high heat. Once the oil shimmers, add the onion and carrots and saute just until they begin to take on color, approximately 3 to 4 minutes. Add the garlic and stir to combine. Add the ground meat, salt and pepper and cook until browned and cooked through, approximately 3 minutes. Sprinkle the meat with the flour and toss to coat, continuing to cook for another minute. Add the tomato paste, chicken broth, Worcestershire, rosemary, thyme, and stir to combine. Bring to a boil, reduce the heat to low, cover and simmer slowly until the sauce is thickened slightly.

Add the corn and peas to the meat mixture and spread evenly into baking cups.

Potatoes:

Top with the mashed potatoes (cooked as you normally make them) and whipped sweet potatoes (I whipped mine with a small amount of real maple syrup).  Note:  I used a cookie gun to pipe the potatoes onto the meat mixture, but spooning it on works just fine. 

Bake for 25 minutes or just until the potatoes begin to brown. Remove to a cooling rack for at least 15 minutes before serving.

This recipe is a sidebar to our larger HALLOWEEN POST from earlier this week, and was inspired by Gina Homolka’s individual meatloaf “cupcakes” at skinnytaste.com.

From-Scratch Mini Meat Pies

Whole wheat pie crusts filled with seasoned ground turkey and vegetables; watermelon balls; whole peach

Whole Wheat Pie Crust (makes 6 minis with room for error):

Blend together 1/4 cup olive oil and 3 tablespoons of water until creamy.  We used a hand-mixer.  In a separate bowl, measure 1 cup of whole wheat flour minus 1 tablespoon.  Add 1 tablespoon of corn starch (to make it closer to a pastry flour). Pour oil/water mixture over dry ingredient mix and knead. Roll out thin layer of dough between waxed paper as if you’re making a full sized pie crust.  Next, punch out 6 round muffin tin sized discs using a large drinking glass (approximately 4 inches in diameter).  Punch out 6 smaller discus for the tops.  Grease muffin tin with preferred method, then press larger discs firmly into tins, fill and cover with smaller discs.  Feel free to get fancy by adding crimps and/or slotted vents in the dough.  My husband (who handles anything dough related) wasn’t feeling fancy late on a Sunday night. 

The Filling:

  • 3/4 pound ground lean turkey
  • 1/3 of a medium onion, peeled and grated
  • 1/2 a large carrot, peeled and grated
  • 1 teaspoon kosher salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon freshly ground black pepper
  • 3/4 of a teaspoon of fennel
  • a little under 1/2 a teaspoon of freshly ground black pepper
  • 2/3 of a cup bread crumbs
  • 2 tablespoons of bran
  • 2 eggs, beaten

1. Preheat oven to 375°F.

2. In a large bowl, combine all the ingredients and 1 tablespoon of the eggs.

3. Spoon a tablespoon and a half of filling per cup into the tin. Cover with the 2-inch rounds on top, pinching the edges together to seal.

4. Poke a hole in the center of each pie.

5. Brush with the reserved egg and bake until the tops are browned and puffed slightly, 30 to 35 minutes.

Serve warm.

Brown Bagging It (Field Trip Day)

Today’s lunch is nitrate/antibiotic-free turkey breast slices on half of a whole wheat croissant; plum slices; fresh blueberries; dried mango and low-sugar almond clusters, all tied in Seventh Generation napkin parcels and secured with raffia ribbons and placed in a labeled brown bag

The girls, who are on a restrictive diet due to familial hypercholesterolemia, have been craving croissants like mad lately.  Since we indulged in holiday treats over Easter, the dietary compromise was to split the croissant (which is from a bakery and is whole wheat and lower in fat than a traditional croissant) between the 2 girls and load up on fruits for today’s brown bag lunch.

Bon appetit!

Spaghetti & Meatballs

Lean herb-seasoned turkey meatballs and no-sugar-added/low-sodium tomato basil sauce over whole wheat spaghetti with a dusting of vegan parmesan cheese; spring salad mix; fresh pineapple chunks; honeydew melon balls

For the turkey meatballs, we used our archived meatloaf recipe and added garlic and Italian herbs.  Turkey meatballs are delicious and *moist* when cooked correctly, never dry. 

To vary the repertoire, this recipe looks tasty and economical (I love the idea of adding bulgar wheat), though you’d probably want to consider substituting lean turkey sausage for the hot Italian sausage on a low-fat diet.
